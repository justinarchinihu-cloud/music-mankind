Newly Installed, Newly Lost

By Kade Lewis — April 19, 2026

We didn’t announce it, didn’t recognize it as it was happening. We moved through spaces the same way, as if we both had seen works similar around us before, or that we are “familiar with this style.”

People became exhibits without us saying so. A laugh across the room, a couple arguing in hushed tones with daggered eyes, someone staring too long at their phone, we would notice, and then look at each other, not to confirm what we saw, but because we already knew we saw it the same way.

It felt like walking through a museum no one else realized they were inside of.

Moments framed themselves, and we wandered through them together, unhurried, observant, detached from it, while growing closer together.

You once told me that most people never really look at anything. That they pass by entire lives the way you pass by paintings in a hallway you’ve already decided you don’t care about.

I remember agreeing, but only because you said it first. I didn’t need to think about it.

That was the point.

With you, understanding didn’t feel like work, it always felt like recognition.

We didn’t just see things. We saw them and read their descriptions to one another.

And for a while, I thought that meant we saw and read each other too.

But something shifted, quietly enough that I couldn’t point to it when it happened.

Maybe it was gradual. Maybe it was always there.

You started explaining things to me that you used to just know I understood.

The way your words began to sound rehearsed, like you had tried them on before handing them to me.

I started to feel like I was being shown something instead of sharing it.

I ignored it.

It felt impossible that the one person who moved through the world like I did could suddenly stop.

But then I noticed how often you looked away before I could meet your eyes.

How your observations became statements, final and unmoving, instead of something we could walk around together.

It wasn’t that you changed all at once.

You allowed yourself to be worked on.

All of a sudden it was them in exhibition, and me standing behind the barrier looking in.

As still and silent as a picture on the wall.

I felt myself get so close my breath fogged up the glass.

I needed you to move, but nothing quivered.

We once were spectators, walking the halls of life’s museum together.

Now my dear friend was a petrified piece of imposed design.

My fist balled up, ready to free them from their enclosure with force.

It hurt me, however they were already installed, displayed, and worse unreachable.
